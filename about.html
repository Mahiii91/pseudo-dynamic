Pasargad: the first imperial capital

Pasargad was the first capital of the Achaemenid Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great in the 6th century BCE. It contained royal palaces, gardens, and a simple but powerful tomb built for Cyrus.

The palace complex was used for royal receptions, religious ceremonies, and the king’s private audiences with nobles and envoys.

Persepolis: the ceremony city

Persepolis, also known as Takht-e Jamshid, was the grand ceremonial capital built later by Darius I and Xerxes. It became the stage for New Year ceremonies, tribute offerings, and state festivals.

The famous Apadana audience hall, the Gate of All Nations, and the throne room were designed to impress and signal the power of the Persian kings.

Gate of All Nations

A massive entrance hall for foreign guests. It represented the king’s control over many peoples and served as the first ceremonial stop for visiting delegations.

Apadana

The great audience hall where the king received gifts and greeted ambassadors. Its walls show processions of tribute-bearers from across the empire.

Royal Guard

Stone sculptures of guards show the palace’s protective presence. These figures reminded visitors that they stood in the king’s controlled sacred space.
